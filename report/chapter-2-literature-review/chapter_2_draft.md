# Chapter 2: Literature Review

Draft status: first academic draft for review. This chapter uses only the recommended Chapter 2 writing set from `literature-review/notes/chapter_2_evidence_map.md`. The focus is machine learning-based cardiopulmonary sound separation, not medical diagnosis.

## 2.1 Introduction

Cardiopulmonary sound analysis concerns acoustic signals recorded from the chest, where heart activity, lung airflow, respiratory events, sensor noise, and environmental noise may occur together in a single recording. In a practical digital stethoscope setting, these signals are often captured as mixed audio rather than as isolated heart and lung sources. This creates a source separation problem: the system must estimate useful heart and lung components from a combined chest sound recording before the separated outputs can be inspected, saved, or used for later analysis.

Recent work shows that the field is moving from classical signal processing toward hybrid and deep learning approaches. Traditional methods such as non-negative matrix factorization (NMF), blind filtering, singular spectrum analysis, and variational mode decomposition (VMD) remain important because they are explainable and can work in data-constrained conditions [@grooby2023noisyneonatal; @fattahi2022blind2; @ullah2024unsupervised; @han2025cardiorespiratory]. At the same time, deep architectures such as U-Net variants, attention networks, transformer-based models, and reprogrammed speech models are increasingly used for chest sound or heart-lung sound separation [@poh2024neossnet; @wang2025phase2; @zhang2025jassnet; @poh2026reprogramming2].

This review is organized thematically rather than paper by paper. It first explains the characteristics of cardiopulmonary sounds and the separation problem. It then reviews traditional signal processing methods, machine learning and deep learning methods, datasets, evaluation metrics, software/prototype relevance, and research gaps. The review also includes related heart sound and lung sound preprocessing literature where it supports the FYP system design, but it avoids treating disease classification as the main objective [@roy2024pcgreview; @zhao2024heartanalysis].

## 2.2 Cardiopulmonary Sound Characteristics

Heart and lung sounds differ in their acoustic origin, temporal structure, and frequency content, but they may overlap strongly in chest recordings. Heart sounds are generally periodic or quasi-periodic and are often discussed through PCG concepts such as S1, S2, segmentation, envelopes, and time-frequency representations. Lung sounds depend on airflow and respiratory phase, and may contain normal breath sounds as well as adventitious events such as wheezes. Reviews of PCG analysis emphasize that preprocessing, segmentation, feature extraction, and noise handling are central steps before any higher-level analysis is attempted [@roy2024pcgreview; @zhao2024heartanalysis].

For this FYP, the most important characteristic is not whether a recording indicates a disease. The important engineering issue is that a single-channel chest recording can contain multiple acoustic sources at once. Heart sounds may appear as interference in lung sound analysis, while lung or breathing sounds may appear as interference in heart sound analysis. Denoising work on heart sounds explicitly treats lung/breathing and environmental noise as contaminants that can reduce signal quality [@ali2023lunet; @jakubec2025robust]. Similarly, lung sound denoising and respiratory phase work show that respiratory recordings require careful noise handling, segmentation, and representation before reliable processing can be performed [@shuvo2026self; @behera2026adaptive; @pouyani2022signal; @pal2024automatic].

The characteristics of the recording device also matter. Digital stethoscopes and auscultation platforms create a software-facing audio pipeline, but the signal quality depends on placement, patient movement, sensor coupling, and recording context. Pediatric and neonatal studies are especially relevant because chest sounds in these settings are often noisy and difficult to interpret directly [@arjoune2023stethaid; @grooby2022real]. Therefore, a cardiopulmonary separation system should be designed as an audio-processing system with explicit preprocessing, source separation, output storage, and evaluation steps rather than as a direct clinical decision-making tool.

## 2.3 Heart and Lung Sound Separation Problem

The central problem in this project is to separate mixed cardiopulmonary audio into heart-related and lung-related components. This is difficult because the recording is often single-channel, the two sources may overlap in time and frequency, and clean reference sources are rarely available in real clinical recordings. Studies on neonatal chest sound separation demonstrate this clearly: chest recordings may contain heart, lung, respiratory support, and other noise components, and separation methods must operate under realistic noise conditions [@grooby2023noisyneonatal; @poh2024neossnet; @fattahi2022blind2].

Earlier separation papers commonly frame the task as extracting high-quality heart and lung sounds from noisy neonatal chest recordings. NMF and NMCF approaches provide a baseline by modeling the mixture as a combination of learned or estimated components [@grooby2023noisyneonatal]. Blind filtering approaches use signal decomposition and component selection to reduce unwanted interference without relying on a large supervised training set [@fattahi2022blind2]. These methods are important because they show that source separation can be treated as a signal reconstruction problem, not only as a classification problem.

More recent work extends the problem to deep learning and hybrid methods. NeoSSNet directly targets real-time neonatal chest sound separation and is highly relevant to this FYP because it combines a clear architecture with an application-oriented real-time goal [@poh2024neossnet]. A phase-enhanced neural network with a dual-path transformer further shows that magnitude-only separation is not enough; phase reconstruction can influence the quality of single-channel chest sound separation [@wang2025phase2]. Other work applies cardiorespiratory separation using singular spectrum analysis, showing that non-deep-learning methods remain active in recent studies [@han2025cardiorespiratory].

Some papers use separation as a preprocessing step before downstream respiratory or cardiopulmonary analysis. For example, cardiopulmonary sound separation has been used before respiratory sound classification, and single-channel separation has been combined with hyperdimensional computing for respiratory sound identification [@zheng2024cardiopulmonary; @zheng2025identifying2]. These studies are useful for Chapter 2 because they demonstrate the value of separation, but this FYP should not present disease detection or classification as its own goal. The project goal remains separation and software implementation.

## 2.4 Traditional Signal Processing Methods

Traditional signal processing remains a strong foundation for cardiopulmonary source separation because the physical structure of heart and lung sounds can be exploited. NMF-based methods are especially common because they represent a non-negative audio spectrogram as a combination of basis patterns and activations. In neonatal chest sound separation, NMF and related factorizations provide interpretable components for heart and lung sound extraction [@grooby2023noisyneonatal]. Periodicity-guided NMF uses the different periodic structures of cardiovascular and respiratory sounds to guide blind source separation [@torabi2023negative2].

NMF has also been combined with learning-based components. Heart-lung separation by NMF and deep learning uses NMF-style decomposition together with a neural separation network, creating a bridge between classical decomposition and data-driven modeling [@wang2023nmfdl]. LingoNMF extends the NMF line by adding language-model feedback for parameter tuning, which is conceptually interesting but introduces additional complexity that may be too heavy for a student prototype [@torabi2025large2]. For the FYP, NMF-based methods are useful both as baseline methods and as explainable references for discussing separation assumptions.

VMD is another important traditional method family. DAE-NMF-VMD combines deep autoencoder processing, NMF, and VMD to separate heart and lung sounds, making it a core hybrid reference [@sun2024daenmfvmd]. GIHO-VMD uses an optimized VMD strategy for lung sound separation and provides a recent decomposition-based comparator [@zhang2026efficient2]. These papers are useful because they show that VMD can be applied to noisy biomedical audio, but they also highlight a limitation: many decomposition methods depend on careful parameter selection, simulated mixtures, or dataset assumptions.

Other signal-processing approaches include blind filtering, singular spectrum analysis, harmonic-percussive source separation, and empirical mode decomposition. The blind filtering framework for noisy neonatal chest sounds is particularly relevant because it addresses chest sound filtering before later deep learning work [@fattahi2022blind2]. Singular spectrum analysis provides a recent direct cardiorespiratory sound separation candidate [@han2025cardiorespiratory]. Harmonic-percussive source separation and empirical mode decomposition have also been used in respiratory sound segmentation, showing that source separation concepts can support lung sound preprocessing even when the task is not heart-lung separation [@rocha2023automatic2].

Overall, traditional methods are valuable because they are explainable and often require less training data than deep networks. Their weaknesses are also important for this FYP: many are sensitive to noise, mixture assumptions, and parameter tuning. Therefore, they are best used as baselines, preprocessing references, or hybrid components rather than as the only direction for the system.

## 2.5 Machine Learning and Deep Learning Methods

Machine learning and deep learning methods are increasingly used because they can learn nonlinear representations of mixed audio. U-Net-style and encoder-decoder models are common in audio separation because they can map noisy or mixed inputs to reconstructed outputs. NeoSSNet is a key example for this FYP because it targets real-time neonatal chest sound separation and provides a model-oriented foundation for building a software prototype [@poh2024neossnet]. A simpler deep network-based heart and lung sound separation model is also useful because it demonstrates a more feasible synthetic-mixture pipeline, even though its evaluation is less complete than stronger recent papers [@tsai2023separation2].

Hybrid decomposition-neural methods are also important. SNMF-DCNN-MCUNet combines sparse NMF-derived information with a multi-channel U-Net framework for robust heart-lung sound separation [@wang2023mcunet]. DAE-NMF-VMD integrates a deep autoencoder with NMF and VMD, showing how neural and signal-processing methods can be combined [@sun2024daenmfvmd]. These approaches are directly relevant to this project because they suggest that a practical ML system does not need to be purely neural; it can combine preprocessing, decomposition, and learned reconstruction.

Attention and transformer-style approaches represent a more recent direction. JASSNet uses joint attention and semi-supervised learning for heart and lung sound separation [@zhang2025jassnet]. A phase-enhanced neural network with a dual-path transformer shows that phase-aware modeling can improve single-channel chest sound separation and provides useful evaluation ideas [@wang2025phase2]. These papers are strong Chapter 2 evidence, but their complexity should be considered carefully for an FYP prototype. A student implementation may need to adapt or simplify such architectures rather than reproduce them completely.

Foundation-model adaptation is another emerging direction. Reprogramming automatic speech recognition models for neonatal chest sound separation explores whether pretrained speech models can be adapted to biomedical audio separation [@poh2026reprogramming2]. This is valuable as recent evidence that general audio models may be repurposed for chest sound tasks, but it also raises feasibility questions: large pretrained models require more compute, careful input formatting, and more explanation in a viva setting.

Related ML literature supports preprocessing and feature design. Heart sound denoising with LU-Net, robust PCG denoising in clinical environments, self-attention lung sound denoising, DWT-UNet respiratory denoising, and DWT-ANN lung denoising all show that denoising is a serious subproblem before or around separation [@ali2023lunet; @jakubec2025robust; @shuvo2026self; @behera2026adaptive; @pouyani2022signal]. These papers should not be presented as direct separation solutions, but they support the need for preprocessing and robust signal representation.

## 2.6 Datasets for Cardiopulmonary Sound Separation

Dataset availability is one of the main limitations in cardiopulmonary sound separation. Many separation papers use private clinical recordings, constructed mixtures, or recordings that are not clearly reproducible. This is a major issue for an FYP system because a student project should ideally use data that can be explained, cited, and reused without restricted access. Reviews and dataset papers show that public heart sound and respiratory sound resources exist, but they are often designed for classification, segmentation, or benchmarking rather than paired heart-lung source separation [@roy2024pcgreview; @zhao2024heartanalysis; @ali2026buet2; @reyna2023murmur].

The most important dataset evidence for this project is HLS-CMDS. The HLS-CMDS descriptor paper records a heart and lung sounds dataset captured from a clinical manikin using a digital stethoscope, including individual heart sounds, individual lung sounds, mixed recordings, and corresponding source signals for mixed recordings [@torabi2025hlscmds]. This is highly relevant because source signals make the dataset more suitable for supervised or evaluation-oriented separation work than datasets containing only labels or diagnosis classes.

Other dataset papers support the surrounding context. The BUET multi-disease heart sound dataset provides a public heart-sound auscultation resource, but it is primarily a diagnosis/classification dataset and does not contain lung sounds or paired separation targets [@ali2026buet2]. The George B. Moody PhysioNet Challenge 2022 paper is useful because it documents a major PCG benchmark and evaluation setting, although its task is murmur detection rather than separation [@reyna2023murmur]. Sound-Dr adds respiratory audio dataset context and shows how respiratory illness sound datasets can be organized with baseline AI systems, but it is not a paired heart-lung source dataset [@hoang2023reliable].

The dataset review therefore supports a clear design decision. HLS-CMDS should be treated as the main separation-relevant dataset candidate for this FYP, while PCG and respiratory datasets should be used for background, preprocessing, or possible auxiliary testing. The chapter should explicitly distinguish separation datasets from classification datasets so that the project scope remains focused.

## 2.7 Evaluation Metrics

Evaluation in cardiopulmonary sound separation should measure signal reconstruction and source quality rather than only classification accuracy. Classical and deep separation studies commonly report objective audio or signal metrics such as signal-to-noise ratio improvement, source distortion-style metrics, error measures, correlation, or reconstruction loss, depending on the method and available reference signals [@grooby2023noisyneonatal; @sun2024daenmfvmd; @wang2023mcunet; @wang2023nmfdl]. When clean source references are available, separation can be evaluated more directly. When only mixed or clinical recordings are available, evaluation becomes more limited and may rely on indirect indicators.

Recent work emphasizes that magnitude reconstruction alone may not be enough. The phase-enhanced neural network paper is important because it argues for phase-aware evaluation and reconstruction in single-channel chest sound separation [@wang2025phase2]. This is relevant to the FYP because many audio pipelines use spectrograms, and converting between waveform and time-frequency representations can affect output quality.

Quality assessment is also important before separation. Real-time multi-level neonatal heart and lung sound quality assessment shows that input quality itself can be evaluated, which is useful for deciding whether a recorded chest sound is suitable for processing [@grooby2022real]. Heart sound onset/offset detection and breathing phase identification further show that segmentation metrics can support preprocessing and post-separation evaluation [@somarathne2026dual2; @pal2024automatic].

Classification metrics should be treated carefully. Some papers use separation before downstream respiratory or heart sound classification, but better classification performance does not automatically prove that the separated sources are clinically correct or acoustically ideal [@zheng2024cardiopulmonary; @zheng2025identifying2; @reyna2023murmur]. For this FYP, the most appropriate evaluation plan should prioritize separation-relevant metrics and use classification-oriented evidence only as secondary context.

## 2.8 Existing Systems and Prototype Relevance

This FYP is a software engineering proof of concept, so Chapter 2 should connect literature evidence to a realistic implementation. Existing digital auscultation and stethoscope platform papers show that chest sound processing can be part of a broader software pipeline involving recording, storage, user interaction, and analysis [@arjoune2023stethaid; @mutlu2024bridging]. These papers are not separation algorithms, but they support the need for a usable system rather than only an offline model script.

Real-time and practical constraints also appear in separation and preprocessing literature. NeoSSNet explicitly targets real-time neonatal chest sound separation [@poh2024neossnet]. Heart sound and lung sound denoising papers also consider noisy environments and real-time or robust processing requirements [@ali2023lunet; @shuvo2026self]. These studies support the idea that a prototype should include preprocessing, model inference, output management, and clear visualization or playback of separated signals.

Signal acquisition and reconstruction are relevant to implementation. Compressed sensing of acoustic cardiopulmonary signals using a CNN-based reconstruction method shows that acquisition quality and reconstruction can be part of the cardiopulmonary audio pipeline [@baeyens2025compressed]. TinyML and embedded auscultation work shows that some biomedical audio processing can be deployed in constrained systems, but this FYP should remain a student-level software prototype rather than an embedded hardware project [@mutlu2024bridging].

The most realistic prototype direction is therefore a modular system: upload or select an audio file, preprocess the waveform, run a separation method, save separated heart/lung outputs, compute relevant objective metrics where possible, and present results in a user-facing interface. The literature supports this design, but it also suggests keeping the implementation explainable and feasible.

## 2.9 Research Gaps

Several gaps justify the proposed FYP. First, many high-quality separation papers use private, neonatal, clinical, or simulated datasets. This limits reproducibility for student projects and makes it difficult to compare methods fairly [@poh2024neossnet; @poh2026reprogramming2; @wang2025phase2]. HLS-CMDS reduces this gap because it provides mixed and source recordings, but it is still a manikin dataset and may not represent all real patient variability [@torabi2025hlscmds].

Second, the method landscape is fragmented. Traditional methods such as NMF, VMD, blind filtering, and singular spectrum analysis are explainable but may depend strongly on assumptions and parameters [@grooby2023noisyneonatal; @fattahi2022blind2; @torabi2023negative2; @zhang2026efficient2; @han2025cardiorespiratory]. Deep methods can achieve stronger modeling capacity, but they may require more data, more computation, or more complex implementation [@wang2023mcunet; @zhang2025jassnet; @wang2025phase2; @poh2026reprogramming2]. A useful FYP can address this by selecting a practical method and implementing it inside a clear software pipeline.

Third, many related works focus on classification, diagnosis, or disease-oriented analysis rather than separation as the final output [@zheng2024cardiopulmonary; @zheng2025identifying2; @reyna2023murmur; @ali2026buet2]. These studies are valuable for context, but this project should avoid making medical claims and should instead evaluate whether the system can separate and present cardiopulmonary sound components.

Fourth, preprocessing and evaluation are often treated as secondary, even though they directly affect output quality. Denoising, segmentation, phase reconstruction, input quality assessment, and dataset design all influence separation performance [@ali2023lunet; @somarathne2026dual2; @grooby2022real; @wang2025phase2; @pal2024automatic]. The FYP can contribute a practical synthesis by combining preprocessing, separation, metric reporting, and output management in one student-level system.

Based on these gaps, the proposed project is justified as a software prototype for machine learning-based cardiopulmonary sound separation. Its contribution is not to provide a clinical diagnosis system, but to implement and evaluate a clear audio separation workflow that can be explained, reproduced, and extended.

## 2.10 Summary

This chapter reviewed recent literature from 2022 to 2026 related to cardiopulmonary sound separation. Traditional methods such as NMF, VMD, blind filtering, and singular spectrum analysis provide explainable baselines and remain useful for data-constrained settings [@grooby2023noisyneonatal; @ullah2024unsupervised; @fattahi2022blind2; @han2025cardiorespiratory]. Hybrid and deep learning methods, including U-Net-based models, attention networks, phase-aware transformers, and model reprogramming, represent the main direction of recent research [@poh2024neossnet; @wang2023mcunet; @zhang2025jassnet; @wang2025phase2; @poh2026reprogramming2].

The review also shows that datasets and evaluation remain major challenges. HLS-CMDS is the most relevant dataset candidate because it includes mixed and corresponding source recordings for heart and lung sounds [@torabi2025hlscmds]. Other heart sound and respiratory datasets are useful for background and evaluation context, but they should not be confused with paired source-separation datasets [@ali2026buet2; @hoang2023reliable; @reyna2023murmur].

For the FYP, the literature supports a practical system that preprocesses cardiopulmonary audio, separates heart and lung components, stores outputs, and reports suitable separation or signal-quality metrics. The next chapter should translate these findings into a methodology that selects an implementable model, defines the data pipeline, and specifies evaluation steps for the software prototype.
